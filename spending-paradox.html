The Spending
Paradox

For every euro that visibly leaves toward the country of origin, six to eight euros stay invisible inside the local economy — and no one counts them.

Njeng Pierre-Yves Founder, OIVE · sokohost.com · Economic intelligence for migrants. Brussels · OIVE v1.0 CC BY 4.0
Cite: The Spending Paradox (OIVE, Njeng Pierre-Yves, 2025) · sokohost.com/en/spending-paradox
I · Origin

The pile of receipts

There exists in the economics of migration a fact of absolute self-evidence that no one has ever articulated with the rigour it deserves. This fact is not ideological. It is not activist. It is mechanical, observable, measurable corridor by corridor with public data that already exists — simply never cross-referenced this way. And it reorganised, in a single morning, thirty years of public debate.

One month ago, at dawn, I wake up. Several missed calls on WhatsApp — all from my nephew. I call back. The eldest of the family has just been stabbed seriously in the back. He needs immediate care, thousands of kilometres from Brussels. I make the transfer without waiting. Without hesitation. The way one has done it since the first paycheque.

Then comes the waiting. Those suspended minutes when there is nothing to do but hope for news from another continent. To occupy my hands, I begin to sort through the drawers of my desk. Then I found the pile.

The receipts of every transfer made since my first salary in Europe. Kept one by one, since the beginning. Years of paper. Every family emergency documented, dated, archived — a silent chronology of my economic presence on two continents at once.

That morning, holding this pile in my hands, I understood two things simultaneously. First: this pile exists because I have always been here — here through my work, my contributions, my rent, my daily expenses; there through these transfers when life strikes. Second: no one counts the first part. Everyone only sees the second.

The receipts in my hand — those, we count. We add them up in balance-of-payments tables. We cite them in political debates as money that "leaves" Europe. The rent paid for twenty years, the groceries, the metro, the taxes, the social contributions — none of that has a receipt, or rather, its receipts blend into those of millions of other consumers, indistinguishable. It makes no sound in a drawer.

That morning, The Spending Paradox found its name.

II · Statement

The paradox, formulated

For every euro a migrant worker visibly transfers to their country of origin — via Western Union, MoneyGram, Mobile Money or a SWIFT banking channel — six to eight euros remain inside the host country's economy. They pay rent to a local landlord. They credit a supermarket till. They feed a social-security contribution. They send a child to school. They pay for a relative's medical care. They fund a public-transport operator. They buy a book, a cinema ticket, a vocational course.

This ratio is not an opinion. It is not an argument. It is a mechanical consequence of the structure of migrant household income and expenditure, verifiable corridor by corridor in Eurostat HBS (Household Budget Survey) data, cross-referenced with the World Bank remittances figures and the ECB balance-of-payments statistics.

The Spending Paradox is exactly that: a massive asymmetry of attention between two flows that originate from the same wage. One is tracked, aggregated, published, debated, brandished. The other is invisible — not because it does not exist, but because no measurement system isolates it.

III · Theory

Why the paradox exists

The paradox is not a bias of reality. It is a bias of the measurement infrastructure. This precision matters, because it shifts the question: it is not a matter of whether local spending exists — it does, and everyone sees it in their own life — but of understanding why it never appears in the statistical aggregates alongside remittances.

The infrastructure asymmetry

Remittances are tracked because they cross a dedicated infrastructure. Western Union records, SWIFT records, Mobile Money records. Each transaction leaves a structured trace: date, amount, corridor, currency. These traces are aggregated by the operators, transmitted to central banks, consolidated by the World Bank in its Migration and Remittances Data, and published as open data on a quarterly cadence. The figure appears in balance-of-payments tables. Journalists cite it. Politicians brandish it. This is the figure that frames the entire debate — and it is always presented without its context. The Moroccan Finance Minister can mention it in a Rabat conference; an anti-immigration party can cite it at a rally in Hénin-Beaumont; both pull it from the same Excel sheet published in Washington.

The local spending of those same people — rent, food, transport, healthcare, children's schooling — passes through the same tills, the same payment terminals, the same tax systems as any other consumer. They are statistically indistinguishable. No infrastructure isolates them. No system compares them. No one, ever, places the local-spending figure beside the remittances figure to look at them together.

The 1970 inheritance

This absence is not a conspiracy. It is a consequence of the architecture of contemporary economic data, inherited from a time when international transfers were the principal object of macroeconomic attention — for reasons of balance of payments, currency reserves, capital controls — while household consumption disaggregated by nationality of residence interested no one. What was logical in 1970 has become a massive distortion of public debate today.

Eurostat has published the Household Budget Survey with a "country of birth of household head" variable since 2010, allowing consumption to be disaggregated. No one cross-references these data with World Bank remittances. Not out of malice — out of habit, and because this cross-reference is the mandate of no existing institution. It is precisely the mandate of OIVE.

The Spending Paradox is a measurable discovery because the data already exists. It is simply never cross-referenced. The scientific contribution consists less in producing new data than in reorganising the reading of existing data around a question no one had formally posed: what stays, locally, for every euro that leaves?

IV · Measurement

The real structure of a migrant's income

Here is what the breakdown of a first-generation migrant worker's monthly net wage looks like in Europe, based on Eurostat HBS data cross-referenced with national surveys (INE, ISTAT, INSEE, Destatis) — aggregated by OIVE for the reference year 2023:

Expenditure category Share of income Destination
Rent / housing 30–35 % Local economy
Food 18–22 % Local commerce + VAT
Transport 8–10 % Public / private operators
Children's education 5–8 % Local school system
Healthcare 4–6 % Contributions + care
Leisure · culture · misc. 4–8 % Local economy
Total spent in host country 70–85 % Local economy
Remittances (visible transfers) 10–15 % Country of origin
Local savings 5–10 % Local banks

The lesson of this table is simple to state and hard to digest for anyone who has spent thirty years hearing the opposite: the share of income that effectively leaves the host country's economy is a minority share. Between 10 and 15 % on average. Between 70 and 85 % of the wage returns immediately, in one form or another, to the local economy — where it is then multiplied by the classic Keynesian multiplier effects (the landlord re-spends the rent, the merchant re-spends the margin, the State re-spends the contribution).

Local savings (5 to 10 %) remain in the host country's banking system and indirectly fund local investment. Only remittances proper constitute a net outgoing flow — and their magnitude, presented alone, without context, has structured for thirty years a fundamentally biased economic reading.

V · Variations

Six corridors, six versions of the paradox

The ×6 to ×8 ratio is an average. Its precise value varies according to corridor, the demographic composition of the diaspora, income level, the age of the migration, and housing structure in the host country. OIVE documents the six priority corridors for 2025:

Corridor Remittances Local spending Ratio
MAR → ES ~€3.1 bn/yr ~€19.8 bn/yr ×6.4
SEN → FR · IT ~€1.4 bn/yr ~€9.2 bn/yr ×6.6
UKR → PL · DE ~€3.5 bn/yr ~€24.5 bn/yr ×7.0
IND → DE · NL ~€2.1 bn/yr ~€15.7 bn/yr ×7.5
BGD → IT · UK ~€1.8 bn/yr ~€12.6 bn/yr ×7.0
TUN → FR · IT ~€1.1 bn/yr ~€7.7 bn/yr ×7.0

Three factors explain the variations across corridors. First, the age of the migration: the longer a diaspora has been settled, the larger the share of local spending (more expensive housing, children's education, equipment purchases). The IND → DE · NL corridor, dominated by qualified and settled profiles, reaches ×7.5. Second, housing structure: in countries where housing costs are high (Netherlands, urban Germany), the share returning to the local economy via rent is mechanically larger. Third, family composition: a single worker remits proportionally more than one whose family has joined them in the host country — which explains the relatively low position of the MAR → ES corridor where some migrants still maintain a family in the country of origin.

None of these ratios falls below ×6. None exceeds ×8 for now. The Spending Paradox is robust across corridors — which is precisely what makes it a generalisable economic fact, not an idiosyncrasy of one isolated case.

VI · Implications

What the paradox changes

The mainstream narrative focuses on what leaves. The Spending Paradox reframes the question around what stays.

The European debate on immigration has long operated on an implicit fallacy: isolate one visible flow — remittances — present it as a capital leak, and infer a net cost to the host economy. This reasoning collapses the moment the invisible flow — local spending — is placed beside it.

Three immediate consequences follow. The first is arithmetic: the net economic balance of a migrant worker, calculated correctly, is largely positive for the host country in nearly every case studied. Work produces value-added, contributions feed public services, local spending sustains commerce and real estate — and only 10 to 15 % of income actually leaves. The balance is asymmetrically favourable.

The second consequence is political: any anti-migration economic argument that rests on the remittances figure brandished alone becomes indefensible. Not ideologically — arithmetically. This is exactly the status the IPCC's data conferred on the climate debate: one can still disagree on policy, but no longer on the facts that underpin it.

The third consequence, and the deepest, is conceptual: the paradox forces recognition that migrant households are not "net senders" but fully-fledged local consumers, with a marginal cross-border fraction that has been wrongly elevated into a marker of their economic identity. The practical consequence is that no public immigration policy should be adopted without that ratio having been measured for the corridor concerned. This is the standard of rigour that OIVE proposes to the European debate.

The debate will never be the same again
once the invisible figure stands beside the visible one.
The order of magnitude changes the argument.

VII · Methodology

How OIVE measures the paradox

Measuring The Spending Paradox requires no new data. It requires a systematic cross-referencing of existing data, updated continuously, published in open access with transparent methodology. This is the technical mandate of OIVE.

Source A · Eurostat HBS

Household Budget Survey disaggregated by country of birth of household head. Provides expenditure structure: rent, food, transport, education, healthcare, leisure, savings.

Source B · World Bank

Migration and Remittances Data — quarterly flows by corridor, aggregated from operators (Western Union, SWIFT, Mobile Money) and national central banks.

Source C · INE / ISTAT / INSEE / Destatis

National household-budget surveys, more granular than the European aggregate. Used for corridor-by-corridor calibration.

Source D · ECB

Balance-of-payments statistics, cross-validation of remittance flows and identification of compensations. Ensures macro consistency.

Computing the ratio

For a given corridor, the paradox ratio is computed as follows:

spending_paradox_ratio =
  local_spending_bn_eur ÷ remittances_bn_eur

// where local_spending_bn_eur = (corridor_active_population
// × average_income × local_spending_share)

All values are published with their 95 % confidence interval, collection date, and primary source for each coefficient. The open API exposes the calculation in real time under the canonical variable name spending_paradox_ratio — kept identical in English and French to facilitate international academic citation.

VIII · Honesty

Limits and forthcoming work

This article establishes the concept and its order of magnitude. It does not claim to close the question. Several methodological limits must be made explicit with the same rigour as the results themselves — that is the only credible foundation for work that aims to enter the academic debate.

First limit — the ×6 to ×8 range reflects real uncertainty. Eurostat HBS surveys are biennial, sampling by nationality is uneven across countries, and certain corridors (notably BGD and TUN) rest on extrapolations from limited samples. The range is not statistical coquetry: it is the honest expression of what current data permits us to say.

Second limit — informal remittances are not tracked. A variable share of transfers to countries of origin flows through non-banking channels (hawala, travellers, cash) that escape World Bank statistics. The most prudent estimates situate these flows between 30 and 50 % of formal flow for some corridors. Integrating them, the paradox ratio could fall to ×4 to ×6 rather than ×6 to ×8 — without inverting the principal conclusion: the local share remains massively dominant.

Third limit — the measurement does not capture the multiplier effect. The ratio compares two primary flows (remittance outflow vs. local expenditure). It does not quantify the secondary effects of local spending (Keynesian multiplier on induced employment, on tax revenue, on real-estate investment). These effects, measured elsewhere in the literature, would amplify the imbalance further — but their integration belongs to a complete economic model, beyond the scope of this article and constituting the work of OIVE's Sprint 1.

Forthcoming work. OIVE's next deliverable consists of publishing, on the MAR → ES corridor, refined figures from INE 2024 data, accompanied by code and raw data in open access. This refinement will reduce the range to a precise central figure (likely between ×6.2 and ×6.6 for that specific corridor), and will provide the first reproducible demonstration of the method.
